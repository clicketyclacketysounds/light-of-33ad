A Physics and Information-Theory White Paper

The Light of 33 AD

Where a Planet's Reflected Record Travels, and What of It Survives

Thought experiment conceived by Paul J. Clark on July 7, 2026.
Researched and compiled by Claude Fable 5 on July 7, 2026.

Working paper, 2026. Quantitative claims follow from the stated inputs or from standard reference values. An asterisk marks a stated assumption or a genuinely uncertain quantity.

Abstract

Sunlight that reflected from Earth in 33 AD has propagated outward for 1,993 years and now forms a spherical shell 1,993 light-years (611 parsecs) in radius, one light-year thick. We treat that reflected light as a physical record of Earth at the instant of reflection and ask two coupled questions: where the shell is now, and how much recoverable information about Earth it still carries. Locating the shell is elementary; the wavefront has already swept past Alpha Centauri, Vega, the Pleiades, and the Orion Nebula, and now crosses the general Cygnus region near 1,800 to 2,000 ly. The information question is governed by four physical filters: inverse-square dilution, Earth-Sun contrast, interstellar extinction, and, decisively, photon shot noise. We find a three-tier result. Planet-level information (atmospheric spectra, a 24-hour rotation signal, seasonal variation, biosignatures) remains recoverable in principle at the wavefront by a sufficiently large collector. Continental-scale geography is marginal and only through a gravitational-lens loophole. Event-level and human-scale information is irretrievably lost, not for want of engineering but because fewer than one reflected photon from such a source crosses any realistic aperture, and no instrument recovers a signal from zero photons. We argue this is dilution past retrieval rather than destruction: under unitary quantum evolution the information disperses into correlations rather than vanishing, a distinction we make precise and bound.

1. Introduction

Consider a familiar fact rendered strange. Earth is not a light source but a reflector: it intercepts a portion of the Sun's output and scatters roughly a third of it back into space in all directions. Every reflected photon that escapes to space departs carrying, in its direction, timing, wavelength, and polarization, some record of the surface, atmosphere, and geometry that produced it. Integrated over the sunlit hemisphere and over time, this outbound flux is a continuously emitted, expanding archive of the planet.

This paper follows one year of that archive. We select 33 AD, a date 1,993 years before the present epoch of 2026, and track the shell of reflected light emitted during that year. Two questions organize the analysis. First, a kinematic one: where is the shell now, and what has it passed? Second, an information-theoretic one: if an arbitrarily advanced but physically constrained observer sat at the wavefront today, what could they in principle reconstruct about Earth in 33 AD, and what is lost beyond any recovery?

The two questions are coupled because the same propagation that carries the record outward also dilutes it. The paper's central contribution is to locate, quantitatively, the boundary between what survives and what does not, and to argue that this boundary is set by hard physical limits (diffraction, contrast, and above all shot noise) rather than by the state of our instruments.

Reference frame.* Distances and the shell's center are given in the Sun's rest frame, treated as effectively inertial over this span. The justification is quantitative: the Sun's galactic-orbit velocity changes the local inertial frame by only about 13 m/s over 1,993 years, negligible against the phenomena considered. In the galactocentric frame the shell stays centered on the solar system's 33 AD position while the solar system itself has since drifted about 1.5 ly (230 km/s times 1,993 yr) from that point. Nearby stars have shifted 0.1 to 0.2 ly from their own peculiar motions of roughly 20 to 30 km/s; real, but small against the distances tabulated below.

Throughout, we hold to three disciplines: every number is derived from stated inputs or cited; distances known to be uncertain (notably Polaris, Betelgeuse, and Deneb) are given only as ranges; and we keep a strict separation between "diluted beyond retrieval" and "destroyed," returning to it in Section 6.

2. The Light Shell Today

2.1 Radius and thickness

Light travels one light-year per year of proper time in vacuum. From 33 AD to 2026 AD is 1,993 years, so the wavefront is 1,993 ly, equivalently 611 pc, from the solar system. Because reflection occurred continuously across the whole of the year rather than in an instant, the shell is not a mathematical surface but an annulus one light-year thick: its inner edge carries light reflected late in 33 AD, its outer edge light reflected early in that year.

2.2 True geometry

The shell is not a uniform luminous sphere. Only the sunlit hemisphere reflects, so at any instant the emission is a lobe filling roughly half the sky as seen from Earth, and that lobe sweeps around the ecliptic once per year as Earth orbits. Superimposed on the annual sweep is a diurnal signal: as the planet rotates, changing arrangements of continent, ocean, and cloud modulate the reflected brightness by 20 to 30 percent over 24 hours, a variation directly measured for the whole-disk Earth by NASA's EPOXI observations. Cloud cover, blanketing roughly two-thirds of the surface at any time, dominates the total albedo. The consequence for information is important and returned to later: the shell is not featureless. It carries, imprinted in its temporal and spectral structure, the phase geometry of an orbiting planet, a rotation period, and weather.

2.3 What the shell has already passed

The arrival year at any object is simply 33 AD plus its distance in light-years. The following objects lie inside the present shell, meaning the 33 AD light has already washed over them (distances are well determined except where flagged):

2.4 The current wavefront

No catalogued object can be pinned exactly at the wavefront, because stellar distances near 600 pc carry 5 to 10 percent measurement errors, a spread of roughly 100 to 200 ly. The front is presently sweeping the general Sadr region of Cygnus near 1,800 ly* and is approaching, in the coming centuries, the Veil Nebula near 2,400 ly (arrival about 2433 AD), the Ring Nebula near 2,570 ly, and the North America Nebula near 2,590 ly. Deneb is a deliberate illustration of honest uncertainty: its published distances span roughly 1,400 to 2,600 ly, so whether the 33 AD shell has already passed it is genuinely unknown.*

2.5 Scale, and a general rule

A sphere of radius 611 pc encloses on the order of 60 million stars* (order-of-magnitude estimate: 0.1 stars per cubic parsec thinned by the disk's roughly 300 pc scale height). The same light will reach the galactic center, 8.18 kpc or 26,700 ly distant, around 26,700 AD, and the Andromeda galaxy in about 2.5 million years.

The rule generalizes to any emission date: the shell radius in light-years equals the current year minus the event year. Humanity's own most famous outbound marker, the sunlight (and faint radio) of the 1969 Apollo 11 landing, forms a shell now 57 ly out, enclosing on the order of 2,200 stars, and it reaches Aldebaran in 2034. A caution belongs here: deliberate and leaked radio emissions are so weak that even our own best instruments could not detect Earth's radio leakage from more than a few light-years away. It is reflected sunlight, not broadcast, that carries the planet's record to interstellar distances.

3. Propagation Effects: Bending, Lensing, and Path

Before asking what a distant observer could recover, we account for what happens to the light in transit. The headline result is that geometry is almost never the problem; photon budget is.

3.1 Gravitational deflection

A ray grazing a mass M at impact parameter b is deflected by the general-relativistic angle alpha = 4GM / (c squared b). Two magnitudes anchor the intuition. Light grazing the Sun's limb bends by 1.75 arcseconds, the classical eclipse measurement. Light grazing an Earth-mass rocky planet bends by only about 0.0006 arcseconds: real, but negligible. The bodies that matter as deflectors are therefore stars, compact remnants, black holes, and the aggregate mass of the galaxy, not planets.

Because deflection falls as 1/b, it weakens rapidly with distance from any given mass. A Sun-like star bends a ray passing at 1 AU by about 8 milliarcseconds, but a ray passing at 1 ly by only about 1e-7 arcseconds. A typical ray crossing 611 pc passes within about 15,000 AU of some star (from n pi b squared L = 1 with n = 0.1 per cubic parsec), where the deflection is around 5e-7 arcseconds. The typical photon, in other words, travels an effectively straight line across the entire 1,993 ly.

3.2 Strong lensing: rare, but the only re-concentrator

Strong lensing, meaning a passage within a star's Einstein radius, is quantified by the optical depth tau = n pi (4GM / c squared) D squared / 6. This yields odds of about 1 in 3e8 for solar-mass lenses along the full path, and about 1 in 7e8 for typical M dwarfs. Even the path's most extreme deflector is modest in absolute terms: a ray passing Gaia BH3 at 1 AU would bend only about 0.27 arcseconds, and the cross-section for so close a pass is minuscule.

The distinction that matters for information is between bending and magnification. Deflection merely redirects a ray; strong lensing can distort or, crucially, magnify. Magnification is significant because a gravitational lens is the only known mechanism that re-concentrates diluted light rather than further spreading it. The Solar Gravitational Lens concept, studied by Turyshev and colleagues under NASA, exploits exactly this: a telescope placed on the focal line beyond 550 AU from a star receives amplification of order 1e10 to 1e11 and could, in principle, image an exoplanet at about 25 km resolution from roughly 100 ly (published and considered physically sound, though not demonstrated). We return to this loophole in Section 5.

3.3 Non-gravitational path effects

Three further effects act on the light, and two of them are information rather than noise. At emission, Earth's atmosphere refracts horizon-grazing rays by up to about 0.5 degrees and imprints a Rayleigh blue tint, polarization, and specular ocean glint; all of these encode real properties of the planet. The interstellar medium does not meaningfully bend optical light; it removes photons through extinction (treated in Section 5.3). Plasma dispersion, which smears broadband signals in a frequency-dependent way, is a radio-frequency concern and is negligible at optical wavelengths.

4. The Resolution Ladder

Suppose photon budget were unlimited. What aperture or interferometric baseline would an observer at 1,993 ly need merely to resolve a given feature on Earth, ignoring for the moment whether enough light arrives? The diffraction limit theta = 1.22 lambda / D at lambda = 550 nm gives a ladder of required scales:

Feature on Earth Angular size at 1,993 ly Aperture or baseline needed
Earth as a resolved disk 139 nano-arcseconds ~1,000 km
5,000-km continental features -- ~2,500 km
A 10-km city -- ~1.27 million km (3.3 Earth-Moon distances)
A 1-meter person -- ~85 AU

The ladder makes a specific point. Resolving continents needs a baseline comparable to a planet's diameter, ambitious but not absurd for a sufficiently advanced interferometer. Resolving a city needs a baseline several times the Earth-Moon distance. Resolving a person needs a baseline of tens of astronomical units. Yet even these are not the fatal obstacle. As the next section shows, the binding constraint is not building a large enough instrument. It is that the photons are not there to be collected.

5. Signal Integrity and Recoverability

We now impose the real photon budget. Standard inputs (standard reference values): solar constant 1,361 W/m squared; Earth radius 6,371 km; Bond albedo 0.30; solar luminosity 3.83e26 W; wavelength 550 nm.

5.1 Inverse-square dilution

Earth intercepts about 1.74e17 W of sunlight and reflects about 5.2e16 W. Spread over a sphere of radius 1,993 ly, this arrives as roughly 1.2e-23 W/m squared, equivalent to about 2.8 reflected photons per square meter per day as an isotropic average. An observer viewing the full sunlit phase receives 8/3 of the average, about 7 photons per square meter per day. A 10-meter telescope, then, collects on the order of 220 Earth photons per day. The light is not gone, but it is extraordinarily thin.

5.2 The contrast problem

Even those photons arrive next to a far brighter neighbor. Earth's reflected output is about 1.4e-10 of the Sun's, the standard exo-Earth contrast figure. At 1,993 ly the maximum Earth-Sun angular separation is about 1.6 milliarcseconds. Splitting the two at bare Rayleigh resolution requires an 85-meter diffraction-limited aperture; a realistic coronagraph with an inner working angle of 2 to 3 lambda/D pushes the requirement to 170 to 250 meters, on top of suppressing the starlight by a factor of 1e-10. Observing in the thermal infrared eases the contrast to 1e6 to 1e7 but demands apertures 18 to 36 times larger. Contrast is a severe constraint, but it is in principle surmountable; it is not the wall.

5.3 Extinction

Interstellar dust removes photons en route. In the galactic plane, extinction runs 0.7 to 1 magnitude per kiloparsec (strongly direction-dependent), which over 611 pc removes 33 to 43 percent of the photons; toward the galactic poles the loss is near zero. Removed photons are not annihilated but scattered out of the coherent image and re-radiated as diffuse glow and thermal infrared. Their imaging information is scrambled even though the photons themselves persist, and because blue scatters more than red, extinction also reddens what remains. This distinction, scrambled versus destroyed, is central to Section 6.

5.4 Spectral shifts

Two shifts are worth checking and dismissing. The Doppler shift from Earth's motion is of order 1e-4, trivial in magnitude, and is in fact a feature: Earth's 30 km/s annual velocity wobble encodes the orbit in a periodic spectral modulation. Cosmological redshift at this distance is z of order 1.4e-7, and since the light never leaves the gravitationally bound Milky Way, even that is not truly accumulated: negligible.

5.5 The information-theoretic result: three tiers

Combining flux, contrast, extinction, and resolution, the recoverability of information sorts cleanly into three tiers.

Tier A, planet-level: recoverable in principle. A collector of 1 square kilometer of effective area gathers on the order of 1.2e5 Earth photons per hour at the wavefront, enough for spectroscopy and for tracking the whole-disk light curve. From this an advanced observer could measure atmospheric oxygen, ozone, and water; detect the vegetation red edge and specular ocean glint; recover the 24-hour rotation period; and register seasonal change. The defensible conclusion available at 1,993 ly is strong: an inhabited, oceanic, photosynthetic planet with a 24-hour day and a 365-day year. Life, in the general sense, is legible in the 33 AD light.

Tier B, geography: marginal, only via the lensing loophole. Direct imaging of continents is photon-starved past hope at this distance. The single escape is the gravitational-lens loophole of Section 3.2. An observer pre-positioned on a stellar gravitational-lens focal line aimed at Earth would receive amplification of 1e10 to 1e11 and could in principle assemble a coarse map. But extrapolating the published Solar Gravitational Lens performance from about 100 ly out to 2,000 ly is a roughly 20-times stretch,* degrading the achievable resolution to a few hundred kilometers and requiring integrations of months to years. Earth's scene changes hourly, so the honest product is a time-averaged, cloud-blurred map of continents at best, not a snapshot.

Tier C, events and people: irretrievably lost, as physics. A single sunlit person reflects about 280 W, roughly 8e20 photons per second. Diluted over the hemisphere at 1,993 ly, that is about 3e-14 photons per square meter per day. To catch even one photon per day from one person requires a collector about 5,800 km on a side, and that single photon arrives buried under about 8e18 solar photons per second, roughly 7e23 per day, striking the same collector. Even a 100-meter city district delivers only about 50 photons per hour to a 1 square kilometer collector, and only out to roughly 1 light-year. The governing principle is unforgiving: when fewer than one signal photon crosses any bounded aperture during an event, no instrument can recover that event, because you cannot beat shot noise with zero photons (a hard quantum limit, not an engineering shortfall).

5.6 Crossover distances

The tiers imply characteristic horizons beyond which each class of information falls below one photon per realistic aperture (order-of-magnitude estimates)*: watchable events around 1 ly; global light curves and weather from tens to a few hundred ly; spectra and life detection out to thousands of ly and beyond; and lens-assisted geography at essentially any distance, given a perfectly pre-positioned observer. The 33 AD shell, at 1,993 ly, sits far outside the event horizon, within the spectral horizon, and at the ragged edge of the lensing loophole.

6. Discussion: Diluted Versus Destroyed

The verdict on the framing question is clean. Could any physically possible observer at the 33 AD wavefront reconstruct events on Earth's surface in that year, a gathering in Jerusalem, a person's face, a single afternoon? No. The event-level photon count across any realistic aperture is less than one, and quantum shot noise makes a sub-photon signal unrecoverable in principle. What survives at 1,993 ly is categorically thinner: that Earth existed, was wet, was alive, rotated in 24 hours, orbited in 365 days, and, through a gravitational lens with luck and precise positioning, a blurred cloud-averaged map of its continents.

It is essential to separate two claims that are easily conflated.

Diluted: yes, catastrophically. By every measure in Section 5, the record is spread so thin that most of it cannot be reassembled by any bounded instrument. This is a statement about retrievability, and it is decisive.

Destroyed: mainstream physics says no. Quantum evolution is unitary; information is not erased but disperses into ever finer correlations among photons, dust grains, and re-emitted heat (with one caveat below). A photon absorbed by an interstellar dust grain is re-radiated as thermal infrared, its original imaging content scrambled into heat in a way that is thermodynamically irreversible in practice, like reconstructing a burned letter from its smoke. The content is scrambled, not annihilated. The deepest form of the question touches the black hole information paradox, where the field has moved strongly toward preservation following the Page-curve results of 2019 to 2020, but that remains an open question in active research.* It barely bears on the present case in any event: only a few rays in a billion are even strongly lensed by a star along the path, let alone swallowed by a black hole.

The upshot is a statement that is both humbling and precise. Roughly half of the shell, the hemisphere of it directed out of the galactic plane, will clear the Milky Way's dust within a few thousand more years and then travel intergalactic space for cosmological timescales (order-of-magnitude). In the most literal physical sense, a faint, diluted, and unreadable record of Earth as it was in 33 AD will outlast the Earth itself.

7. Conclusion

Locating the light of 33 AD is a matter of arithmetic: a shell 1,993 ly in radius and 1 ly thick, having already passed tens of millions of stars and now crossing Cygnus. Reading it is a matter of physics, and physics draws a sharp and defensible line. Planet-level facts survive in principle at the wavefront; continental geography survives only through a gravitational-lens loophole and only as a time-averaged blur; and everything at the scale of a human event is gone past any recovery, foreclosed by shot noise rather than by the limits of engineering. The record is diluted beyond retrieval but, under unitary evolution, never destroyed. That is the paper's one-line result, and it is worth stating plainly: the past is not preserved in a form we could ever watch, yet its faintest physical trace endures beyond the lifetime of the world that cast it.
